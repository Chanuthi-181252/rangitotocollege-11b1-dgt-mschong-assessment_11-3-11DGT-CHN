Conservation:

Interventions

  1. Introduced predators/competitor species - creation of offshore or onshore predator free islands which have predator proof fences and are monitored with cameras, traps, tracking cards and tunnels. (Protected Areas)
  2. Restoration of habitat - replanting the natural habitat so specialist species can feed and reproduce in their original habitat.
  3. Establishing new populations in suitable habitats by translocation.
    • New populations must be large enough population so inbreeding is minimised and founder effect is not too pronounced. Enough breeding pairs must be translocated to prevent inbreeding depression.
    • Populations must be far enough apart so diseases can not be transferred between populations. If one population creashes other popuations ensure the species is not wiped out.
    • Transfer birds between populations after they are established to maintain genetic diversity and to prevent RIMs (Reproductive Isolation Mechanisms) forming (eg. behavioural due to birdsong).
    • Different populations would be exposed to different selection pressures so the allele frequencies of the populations would differ, increasing genetic diversity.
  4. Double clutching increases the number of chicks hatched increasing the reproductive rate. Surrogate species may be used to raise these chicks. The surrogate must be a closey related species as the chicks may imprint the behaviour of the parent.
  5. Hand raising chicks in captivitiy to remove risk factors to allow them to survive until adolescence when they can be released. Adolescents are more likely to survive predation.
  6. DNA screening to identify parents with deleterious alleles - selective breeding - do not allow these birds to breed. DNA screening can also identify genetically diverser individuals to increase outbreeding.
  7. Gene Editing - using Crispr to potentially edit harmful alleles from an individual/population.
  8. Wildlife corridors that maintain gene flow between populations/prevent species crossing highways where they can be killed.
  9. Legislate against any human impacts affecting the vulnerable species (eg. bn hunting etc.)
  10. Vaccinations - vaccines can be developed to help prevent spread of pathogens etc.

Parental Care

r-strategists (ruthless) k-strategists (kind)
Definition Little to no parental care, with large numbers of offspring produced. Large amounts of parental care. Few offspring are born and the parents spend a lot of time raising the young.
Advantages
  • Don't use energy over a long period of time caring for offspring (so fitness of parent does not decrease).
  • Offspring are not dependent on parents for survival.
 Offspring are well fed and protected (so high probability of survival).

Reproduction Rates

These rates are often slow in threated organisms for multiple reasons

  • Time taken to reach sexual maturity - varies between species, but can be a number of years
  • Number of eggs/offspring produced at one time - ususally only 1-2 offspring are produced
  • Frequency of producing offspring - many threatened species do not produce offspring each year
  • Short life span - so fewer offspring produced overall

Intraspecific Competition

Interspecific competition is the competition between organisms/members of the same species.

  • It occurs for the need of resources (food, water, space, shelter & mates)
  • This competition is more intense compared to interspecific competition because individuals of the same species require the same recourse to occupy the same niche

Mating Systems

  • Polygynandry = males mate with multiple females (this creates larger genetic diversity as there is less competition for mates)
  • Monogamy = each individual mates with only one partner

Pair Bonding

Pair Bond = a stable relationship between animals of the opposite sex that ensures cooperative behaviour during mating and rasing the young.
Disadvantages:

  • Offspring are dependent on parents for survival for long period of time
  • Parenting is energy expensive and so breeding is limited to allternate years
  • Offspring is vulnerable if one or both parents die

Recovery Programmes

Successful population recovery programmes aim to:

  • Increase population size
  • Increase genetic diversity
  • Establish self-sustaining populations
  • Have multiple populations (act as insurance in case populations don't survive)
  • Multiple breeding pairs (to continue to increase genetic variation)

These goals ensure the long-term survival and resilience of threatened species, contributing to broader biodiversity and ecosystem health.
