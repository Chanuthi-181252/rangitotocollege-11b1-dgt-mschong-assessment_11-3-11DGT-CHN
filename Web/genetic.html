Genetic Manipulation:

Selective Breeding

Selective breeding is a process where animal and plant breeders select individuals with desireable traits and breed them to produce offspring with these desirable traits.

Implications:

  • Harder to control which combinations of alleles are passed on until it's sure that both parents are homozygous
  • Alleles are lost over generations - these lost alleles may be beneficial in the future. These alleles are lost as the organism that has these alleles have the undesirable allele for the desirable trait
  • Takes several generations
  • Lots of unwanted animals
  • Problems of inbreeding - lethal recessive alleles more likely to come together in an offspring and inbreeding depression may cause the loss of biological fitness over time

Process:

  1. Decide which characteristics are desired
    - Select desired phenotypes
    - Determine whether the desired allele is dominant/recessive

  2. Chose the type of breeding and then parents that show these characteristics
    - Decide whether to inbreed, outbreed or line breed
    - Marker Assisted Selection to identify individuals with the desired combination of alleles or sequences related to desired phenotypes

  3. Carry out the reproductive process
    - Artificial Insemination, Embryo Flushing and Embryo Transfer
    - IVF (In Vitro Fertilisation) and Embryo Transfer

  4. Select the best offspring from parents to breed the next generation

  5. Repeat the process continuously

Cloning

Reproductive cloning is the process by which a whole organism is cloned.

Look at the right for how this process works.

Implications:

  • If successful the resultant animal is guaranteed to have the gene of interest
  • Low success rate / takes time to get a successful clone
  • Some clones are born with organ deformitites
  • As all organisms that are cloned are identical to each other, this means there is no genetic variation

Transgenesis

Transgenesis is a process where the DNA from one species is transferred to another species by artificial means.

Implications:

  • Difficult to successfully insert the gene then successfullly re-implant embryo
  • Possible pleiotropic effects
  • Low success rate / takes time
  • If successful, it can increase the fitness the organism

Process:

  1. Design gene construct by accociating the gene of interest with an appropriate promoter
  2. restriction enzymes - cut the gene of interest, promoter and plasmid with the same restriction enzymes
  3. Ligation - make recombinant DNA by stick the gene construct together
  4. Check the gene construct sequence by DNA sequence analysis
  5. Multiplying the plasmid in bacteria and recovering the cloned construct for injection
  6. Transferrence of the construct into the recipient tissue by micoroinjection, usually into fertilised eggs (In mammals, the egg is implanted into a surrogate)
  7. Integration of gene into recipient genome
  8. Checking offspring is transgenic and for the number of insertion sites by DNA sequence analysis
  9. Expression of gene in recipient genome
  10. Inheritance of gene through further generations

CRISPR

CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) is a powerful editing tool used in genetic engineering. It allows scientists to make precision edits to DNA in living organisms.

Applications:

  1. Medicine & Healthcare
  2. Agriculture
  3. Environmental Science:
    • Ecological Management: CRISPR can help in managing invasive species by altering their genetic makeup to reduce their impact on native ecosystems
    • Conservation: It can be used to enhance the genetic diversity of endangered species or to resurrect traits that hve been lost over time
  4. Basic Research
  5. Agricultural biotechnology:
    • Pest Control: CRISPR is used in creating genetically modified insects that can help in controlling pest populations
    • Plant Breeding: It helps in developing crops with enhanced nutritional profiles, better resistance to diseases, and improved growth charateristics
  6. Gene Drives
