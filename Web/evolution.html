Evolution:

Evolutionary Processes

Evolution is the change in allele frequencies in a population over time. Therefore, evolutionary processes are processes that cause changes in allele frequencies in a population over time.

  • Allele = an alternative version of a gene
  • Allele frequency = the precentage (proportion) or each allele in a gene pool
  • Population = a group of organisms of the same species, which live in the same place at the same time
  • Gene Pool = all the inheritable alleles present (available) in a population
  • Genetic diversity = the variation of heritable traits in the population

Significance/Importance of Genetic Diversity

  • Genetic diversity is important because if there is more genetic variation in a population, it means the population is more likely to be able to survive in a wider variety of environmental conditions and has a greater chance to survive changes in their environment.

Mutations - a permanent change in the nucleotide base sequence of DNA

  • Mutations are the only source of new alleles in individuals and therefore in a gene pool.
  • A new unique allele produced by a mutation will increase the size of the gene pool and therefore the genetic variation in the population, but only if the mutation is inheritable.
  • If the nutation is expressed in the phenotype then the process of natural selection will work to either increase or decrease its frequency in the population.

Gene Flow - is the movement of alleles between populations/gene pools when individuals migrate between populations.

  • Immigration can increase genetic variation because unique alleles may be introduced when immigrants enter a population/gene pool and interbreed, and so it would increase the size of the gene pool.
  • Emigration can lower genetic variation because it removes alleles from the population when individuals leave, and so it can decrease allele frequency or if a qunique allele is removed it would decrease the size of the gene pool.


Selection Pressures - are factors that affect fitness/reproductive sucess and include limits on resources and the existence of threats.

Natural Selection - is the process where environmental factors act as selecting agents and they select for the phenotype that is best suited to the environment. There must be variation in the phenotypes in the population to start with for natural selection to work.

  • Individuals with the phenotype best suited to the environment have a survival advantage (are 'fitter'), so are more likely to survive, and therefore pass on their alleles for the favouralbe phenotype to offspring more often, which means the frequency of the benefical allele(s) increases in the gene pool.

Genetic Drift - is the change in the frequency of an existing allele in a population due to random sampling of individuals through chance events.

  • Founder Effect occurs when a small group of individuals leaves a larger original population and starts a new popultion somewhere else, and because it is a small sample of the origianl population, it is likely the allele frequencies will be different to the original larger population, and so genetic variation will often be reduced.
  • Bottleneck Effect is the sudden reduction in the size of a population due to the loss of a large number of individuals, and because it is a small sample of the origianl population, it is likely the allele frequencies will be different to the original larger population, and so genetic variation will often be reduced.

Evolution

Patterns of Evolution

  • Divergent Evolution
    Divergent evolution is splitting of a common ancestral species into two or more species that develop different phenotypes due to becoming specialised to occupy different ecological niches. They have a common direct ancestor but different selective pressures in the different ecological niches result in natural selection favouring different phenotypes, resulting in different species from the common ancestor over time.

  • Adapative Radiation
    Adaptive radiation occurs when a common ancestral species evolves into a large number of new species (rapid speciation into many different forms) due to the availability of many new ecological niches (so the ancestral species moved into each of these available niches, and so was then exposed to different selection pressures in each niche which changed its phenotype / selected for different adaptations). Availabe niches can be made through extinction events or environmental changes.

  • Convergent Evolution
    Convergent evolution is when two or more species from differenet direct ancestors (they do not share a recent common ancestor) evolve similar phenotypes in response to similar niche requirements/environmental selection pressures.


  • Co-evolution
    Co-evolution occurs when different groups of organisms have close ecological relationships, and so they exert selection pressures on each other (they have a strong influence on each other's evolution), and any change in one species acts as a selection pressure that causes/leads to reciprocal changes in the other species. The species develop specific adaptations to changes that occur in the other so that they can continue their existence in the presence of the other. It is most common when a species depends on the other for survival e.g. predator and prey relationships.

Rates of Evolutionary Change

  • Punctuated Equilibrium
    Key points:
    1. A species stays unchanged for a long period of time
    2. Rapid speciation occurs in a short period of time
    3. This often occurs because of sudden significant environmental change
    4. The transitional species may not be seen in the fossil record
  • Gradualism
    Key points:
    1. The species are undergoing slow adaptive change continuously
    2. This is due to constant action of evolutionary processes and changes in selection pressures acting on them
    3. So new species will appear continually - spread out over the time interval (at different points in time)
    4. The transitional species should appear in the fossil record as it takes a while for the new species to evolve

Speciation

Speciation - The formation of different species from a common ancestor/ancestral species


Species - a group of organisms that can interbreed to produce fertile offspring.


Speciation (the formation of new species) can only occur if:

  • There is genetic variation in the species/population to begin with, then a group of individuals becomes separated from the common original population/species
  • So that there is no gene flow
  • And then evolutionary processes increase the genetic vriation between the group and the common original population/species
  • Until they are different enough that the group of individuals can no longer successfully reproduce with individuals from the common original population/species, and so they are reproductively isolated from the orginal ancestral species

Types of Speciation

  • Allopatric speciation: The formation of different species from a common ancestral species after a common original population becomes divided by a physical barrier into two or more geographically isolated populations
  • Sympatric speciation: The formation of different species from a common ancestral species in the same geographical area without prior geographical isolation (no geographical barrier - so they live in the same area)

RIMs

Reproductive Isolating Mechanism (RIM) = a barrier that prevents two organisms from different species from mating and producing fertile offspring / prevents successful interebreeding / prevents gene flow

  • Geographical Isolation - when physical barriers separate populations so they cannot come into contact to reprodcue and so there is no gene flow
  • Ecological Isolation - individuals of a species live in the same geographical area but differences in habitat/ecological niches results in them rarely coming into contat, so they do not reproduce and so there is no gene flow between them
  • Temporal Isolation - differences in the timing of their behaviour e.g. mating is prevented because the two species breed at different times of the year or day
  • Structural (mechanical) Isolation - structural shape means that mating is not possible
  • Behavioural Isolation - results from differences in behaviour, usually different courtship rituals, so the courship rituals are not recognised and so mating will not occur; or one species might be nocturnal and the other diurnal so they do not come into contact
